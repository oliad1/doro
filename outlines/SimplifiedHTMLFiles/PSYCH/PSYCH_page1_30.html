Course Description

PSYCH 417:

Through training attention via mindfulness, this course provides a deeper personal and general understanding of stress, stress reactivity, adaptive stress responses, and wellness. Experiential learning will be central: students will participate in guided mindfulness practices in class and use the support and structure of the course to develop and maintain a daily mindfulness practice at home. Reflections on personal experience, rooted in both scientific and meditative inquiry, will enrich experiential learning.

Course Content and Experiential Approach to Learning

I am looking forward to embarking on this journey in mindfulness with you this term! This course will likely be quite different from other academic courses you have taken in that it will revolve primarily around an experiential practice: that of mindfulness.¬†

Mindfulness entails ‚Äúpaying attention on purpose, in the present moment, and nonjudgmentally to the unfolding of experience moment by moment‚Äù (Kabat-Zinn, 2003).¬†Although there is no sought-after outcome when we practice mindfulness, the practice of training attention via mindfulness gives rise to greater awareness of our habitual mental, physical, and emotional reactions, including the ways in which these reactions can create stress reactions and various forms of psychological suffering such as those studied and treated in the field of clinical psychology. Mindfulness can also give rise to momentary understandings of how we can experience reduced stress and improved mental health.

Mindfulness practice is an experiential approach, and thus the course will be experiential meaning that we will learn about mindfulness by practicing it ourselves and reflecting on our experiences.¬†The practices and reflections we will engage in derive from the well-established, empirically supported Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) program, developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn. Although I have provided weekly readings for this course on LEARN, these are meant to be secondary to your own direct learning about mindfulness through daily practice. This experiential and less analytical approach to learning may feel foreign and uncomfortable, and you might feel resistant and skeptical. That is okay! These reactions are part of the range of inner experiences we will be paying attention to with curiosity and non-judgment throughout the course.¬†

What to Expect from Class Time and Home Assignments in this Course¬†

Both in-class and at home, students will engage in formal mindfulness practices guided by the instructor or and/or by another meditation instructor via audio recordings. In these practices, you will be guided to pay attention to various aspects of your internal experience, including your breath, body sensations, emotions, thoughts, behavioural urges, and behaviours. These practices will sometimes occur while seated and at other times while engaging in gentle stretch-like movements. Gentle movement meditations can be performed sitting down or standing and certain home practices can be performed lying down if desired. We will begin with shorter practices of roughly 5 minutes together in class, and gradually extend the length of the practices to 20-30 minutes. Sample practices can be found¬†on LEARN.¬†

Class time will typically center around mindfulness practices that we will engage in together as a group. Class will also involve self-reflection and inquiry-based discussion after each mindfulness practice, in smaller groups and as a full class. During the mindfulness practices and at other times specified by the instructor, you will be asked to put away non-essential technology to facilitate deeper learning. Weekly home assignments will primarily revolve around daily mindfulness practice and reflection.

Silent Retreat Class.¬†One of our allotted class times will consist of a ‚Äúsilent retreat‚Äù which is a key element of the Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction program. During this class, the entire three hours will consist of a series of ~20- to 30-minute mindfulness practices, with short breaks interspersed. Students will be asked to be silent throughout this class, and to refrain from all forms of communication and technology during the duration of the class.¬†

Distinguishing this Course from Psychotherapy and Counselling

Not counselling/psychotherapy.¬†Although this course is being taught by a psychologist,¬†this is an academic course, not a form of psychotherapy or a support group, nor is it a substitute for psychotherapy/counselling or medical treatment. If students are experiencing current mental health struggles (e.g., depression, anxiety, trauma symptoms), it is their responsibility to ensure this is an appropriate course for them to take at this time, and they are encouraged to seek guidance and services from a health or mental health professional outside of the course.¬†

Not a form of training to provide counselling/psychotherapy.¬†This course does not provide training in the delivery of psychotherapy or counselling. At the end of this course, students should not¬†consider themselves qualified to offer psychological counselling or to teach mindfulness.

Relationship with course instructor, teaching assistant and other students.¬†Although psychological material will be explored through this course, the relationship between the student and instructor is one of instructor-student and not therapist-client. The same goes for the student-TA relationship. Students who enroll in the course and experience distress should not seek counselling from the instructor or from other classmates but instead should seek out other mental health supports. The instructor is happy to provide recommendations if desired.

Benefits, Risks and Student Suitability

Benefits

There is an extensive research body documenting the benefits of Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) programs, which will comprise the experiential basis for mindfulness practice in Psych 417. ¬†Specifically, systematic reviews of research studies have found that at the end of an 8-week MBSR group program, participants typically report greater physical and mental well-being including reduced stress (self-reported and via physiological markers), anxiety, depression, pain, and distress, and increased quality of life (Grossman et al., 2004;¬†Khoury et al., 2015;¬†Pascoe et al., 2017). Moreover, the benefits participants derive from mindfulness-based programs are greater the more time they spend engaging in at-home mindfulness practices (Parsons et al., 2017). It is nevertheless impossible to guarantee that you will experience personal benefits from the practice of mindfulness through this course.¬†

Risks

The practice of mindfulness involves paying attention to our internal experiences in ways that may feel unfamiliar. When we carve out space and time away from our devices and usual distractions and pay attention to what experiences arise in us, we can often find ourselves surprised by what we notice. We may find we are experiencing emotions, thinking patterns, urges, and physical sensations that we had no idea were present. This greater awareness that mindfulness practice facilitates can sometimes result in feelings of emotional distress and physical discomfort. Indeed,¬†research has found that over the course of mindfulness meditation practice, some individuals may experience adverse events, with the most common being increased anxiety, the re-experiencing of traumatic symptoms, and heightened emotional sensitivity (Goldberg et al., 2021). Although the instructor will provide guidelines for how students can keep uncomfortable emotions and sensations within a personally tolerable range, it is important to note that you will probably experience some emotional and physical discomfort throughout the course.

Strategies to Mitigate Risks.¬†At the start of the course, we will be reviewing what Dr. Dan Siegel termed the ‚ÄòWindow of Tolerance‚Äô which organizes our experience into those that are within vs outside our window of tolerance. When we are within our window of tolerance, we feel grounded, flexible, curious/open, present, and able to emotionally self-regulate. When we are outside our window of tolerance, we may experience hyper-arousal (e.g., high energy, anxiety, anger, overwhelm) or hypo-arousal (e.g., numb, withdrawn, frozen, shut down). In this course, we will be making a commitment to paying attention to how well we are tolerating the experiences arising for us during mindfulness practice and to using strategies to keep us within our windows of tolerance. Each student will also be guided to identify, in advance, signs that might indicate to them that they are outside of their window of tolerance and to devise a list of strategies they can use in those moments to help themselves feel safe. Strategies might include: opening eyes; taking slow breaths with longer exhales; drinking a glass of water; naming five things you can see, hear and feel; standing up and feeling one‚Äôs feet on the ground; focusing on something that feels safe like a particular sound, visual, or part of the body; or mindfully leaving the classroom for a few minutes (while signaling this to the instructor and/or teaching assistant). It should be noted that the students are responsible for attending to and taking care of their own well-being and for connecting with professional counselling and/or crisis supports if they begin to feel emotionally and/or physically unwell during course activities.¬†

Accessibility.¬†Students will be provided with options regarding how to slightly alter practices to accommodate their physical and emotional needs. For example, you will be able to sit rather than stand, if desired, and will be permitted to end a practice early if they feel it is too long to tolerate safely. Students will also be provided with a recommended mindfulness practice to perform at home on a given week (e.g., body scan), but have permission to perform a different practice (e.g., sitting meditation) should they feel unable to commit to the one that is assigned. All practices share the focus on cultivating a particular way of paying attention to our experiences, and so it is okay if a student feels the need to omit one type of practice in favour of another that feels more tolerable for them to engage in. Students will not need to seek instructor permission to make these adjustments.

Student Suitability

Because this course is taking place in an academic, non-clinical setting, students who are currently experiencing any of the following mental health challenges should give careful thought as to whether now is the best time for them to undertake this course. The risks of participating in mindfulness-based programs may be greater for individuals who are presently experiencing the mental health challenges below which is not an exhaustive list (Woods & Rockman, 2021). ¬†

  • Very low mood or depression
  • Severe anxiety
  • Recurrent, strong emotional reactions that are proving difficult to manage
  • Hallucinations (auditory or visual)
  • Suicidal or homicidal thoughts or actions
  • Current symptoms related to trauma like flashbacks
  • Recent traumatic experience
  • Significant drug or alcohol use

If you are experiencing current mental health challenges, it would be wise to discuss your participation in this course with a mental health provider (e.g., therapist, counsellor, psychologist), and ensure you have access to them over the course of your enrollment in the course.

Class Conduct and Communication Guidelines

Mindfulness practices and discussions will take place in-class and thus require that we all be aware of certain guidelines that support everyone‚Äôs physical and psychological safety to the best of our abilities. Although this is not an exhaustive list, and we will be generating our own personalized community guidelines together in class, it is recommended that all students participating in the class do their best to commit to the following guidelines adapted from the international virtual mindfulness practice community, Cloud Sangha:

  • Conduct ourselves in a respectful and conscientious manner, which includes putting away electronic devices when asked to by the instructor and fully listening to others who are speaking.
  • Respect the rights and dignity of the instructor, teaching assistant, and students regardless of their age, race, ethnicity, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, physical or mental ability, country of origin, religion, first language, and so on.
  • Treat any personal information shared in the class, by the instructor and fellow students, as confidential.
  • Commit to authentic, respectful, and mindful communication.
  • Conduct ourselves in a way that encourages open communication.

Appropriate group conduct includes:

  • Sharing from our own personal experience
  • Listening mindfully to what others have shared

Inappropriate group conduct includes:

  • Offering commentary, interpretation, or advice to others
  • Mentioning or discussing self-harm or bringing harm to others
  • Asking the instructor, TA, or classmates for support or help with personal crises
  • Regular absences from class (unless there are extenuating circumstances)

Boundaries around topics of discussion:

  • Because of the academic, non-clinical nature of our course, this class is not a place in which to discuss and try to process active mental illness and trauma. Students who are undergoing these experiences are encouraged to receive support from an outside mental health provider.
  • The course instructor reserves the right to redirect a student elsewhere if she believes that participation in the course may be harmful to the student and/or to other members of the class community (other students, the instructor, the teaching assistant).

Please ensure you review the Information and Consent Letter posted on LEARN.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you should be able to:
1. Engage in mindfulness practices consistently and safely, and incorporate these practices into daily life.
2. Inquire into the experiences that arise in your mind and body, during and outside of formal mindfulness practice.
3. Examine personal cycles of stress reactivity and relate these cycles to scientific models of stress.
4. Embody mindful conduct in a classroom community.
5. Integrate psychological theories and research into your experiential understanding of mindfulness and its relationship to stress reduction.
6. Investigate personal communication patterns and strategies to foster more mindful interpersonal relations.

Assessment/Grading Philosophy

I would encourage you to adopt a learning orientation through this course, rather than a performance orientation. In other words, try to approach this course and the mindfulness practices, reflections and discussions with a relaxed, patient interest in discovery rather than a driven, goal-oriented focus on achieving a certain grade, coming across a certain way in class, and/or experiencing certain benefits. The act of striving toward a particular outcome undermines our capacity to be present to the learning that unfolds naturally and experientially. I have set up the assessment structure to try and support this learning orientation, as outlined below in the Assessment section.¬†

Although you will have a richer experience in this course if you approach it with a learning orientation without a focus on outcomes, this is an academic course in which grades ultimately need to be assigned. I have therefore given careful thought to developing an assessment method that supports the philosophy and experiential learning focus of the course. The focus of assessment will be primarily on your level of mindful engagement with the course which refers to your level of openness to learning, authenticity, and curiosity vis-√†-vis your own personal experiences with mindfulness practices, as well as your attempts to be attentive, respectful, and open in class when speaking, when listening to others, and when engaging in self-reflection.¬†

Because you are most intimately acquainted with your level of curiosity, openness, and engagement in course material, self-assessment will form a key part of the assessment method for the course. Self-assessment will occur at the middle and end of the course rather than every week to remove a focus on micro-analyzing and performance. The instructor and TA will consider the self-assessments you provide and will provide a final grade taking into account your self-assessment and their own assessment taking into account the grading scheme and rubrics, and their review of all students in the course.

Experiential learning is considered an integral part of the undergraduate program in Psychology. Research participation is one example of this, article review is another.¬†A number of undergraduate courses have been expanded to include opportunities for Psychology students to earn grades while gaining research experience.

Since experiential learning is highly valued in the Department of Psychology, students may earn up to 2% of their final mark in this course through research experience (i.e., course work will make up 98% of the final mark and research experience will make up the other 2% for a maximum grade of 100%). The two options for earning research experience grades; participation in research through online remotely operated and In Lab studies, as well as article review; are described below. Students may complete any combination of these options to earn research experience grades. Credits will be permitted to be earned with half from online, and the other half (1.0) from In-lab or Remote Access studies.

Option 1:¬† Participation in Psychology Research

Research participation is coordinated by the Research Experiences Group (REG). Psychology students may volunteer as research participants in remotely operated, In Lab and/or online (web-based) studies conducted by students and faculty in the Department of Psychology. Participation enables students to learn first-hand about psychology research and related concepts. Many students report that participation in research is both an educational and interesting experience. Please be assured that all Psychology studies¬†have been reviewed and received ethics clearance through¬†a University of Waterloo Research Ethics Committee.¬†

How to earn extra marks for your Psychology course(s) this term by participating in studies ...

  • You will earn "credits" which will be converted to "marks" (1 credit = 1%)
  • You can schedule your remotely operated (replacing in-lab) and ONLINE studies using the "Sona" website.

Educational focus of participation in research

To maximize the educational benefits of participating in research, students will receive feedback information following their participation in each study detailing the following elements:

  • Purpose or objectives of the study
  • Dependent and independent variables
  • Expected results¬†
  • References for at least two related research articles
  • Provisions to ensure confidentiality of data
  • Contact information of the researcher should the student have further questions about the study
  • Contact information for the Director of the Office of Research Ethics should the student wish to learn more about the general ethical issues surrounding research with human participants, or specific questions or concerns about the study in which s/he participated.¬†

Participation in remotely operated (counts as the same as in-lab) studies has increment values of 0.5 participation credits (grade percentage points) for each 30-minutes of participation. Option 2: Article Review as an alternative to participation in research

Students are not required to participate in research, and not all students wish to do so. As an alternative, students may opt to gain research experience by writing short reviews (1¬Ω to 2 pages) of research articles relevant to the course. The course instructor will specify a suitable source of articles for this course (i.e., scientific journals, newspapers, magazines, other printed media). You must contact your TA to get approval for the article you have chosen before writing the review.¬†Each review article counts as one percentage point. To receive credit, you must follow specific guidelines. The article review must:

  • Be submitted before the¬†last day of lectures. Late submissions will NOT be accepted under ANY circumstances.
  • Be typed
  • Fully identify the title, author(s), source and date of the article.¬†A copy of the article must be attached.
  • Identify the psychological concepts in the article and indicate the pages in the textbook that are applicable. Critically evaluate the application or treatment of those concepts in the article. If inappropriate or incorrect, identify the error and its implications for the validity of the article. You may find, for example, misleading headings, faulty research procedures, alternative explanations that are ignored, failures to distinguish factual findings from opinions, faulty statements of cause-effect relations, errors in reasoning, etc. Provide examples whenever possible.¬†
  • Clearly evaluate the application or treatment of those concepts in the article.
  • Keep a copy of your review in the unlikely event we misplace the original.

Confidentiality and Privacy

The College of Psychologists includes teaching of a psychological nature by a member of the College to be a ‚Äòpsychological service,‚Äô particularly when there are exchanges between students and the instructor regarding personal information, such as about experiences that arise during the practice of mindfulness. Therefore, the instructor, who is a registered psychologist, is required to keep brief notes about the discussions that take place in the Psych 417 class. The notes will be saved in password-protected documents on the instructor‚Äôs password-protected University of Waterloo server. No one else will have access to these notes and they will be kept confidential except under the circumstances outlined below when the instructor is professionally and legally obligated to breach confidentiality by their College.

  1. If anything makes the instructor believe that you are an imminent danger to yourself or to someone else, they will be required to contact the authorities or others who can intervene to help prevent harm to you or others, and any intended victims of harm;
  2. If the instructor learns that you have been sexually abused by another registered health provider, they will have to report what they know; however, the instructor will not give your name without your written permission;
  3. If the instructor suspects or is informed that a child is at risk of being harmed in any way (e.g., sexual or physical abuse, negligence), they must contact Child and Family Services and report all relevant information;
  4. If the contents of the instructor‚Äôs course notes are ordered by a court of law, the instructor is required to release the file to the court;
  5. If College of Psychologists of Ontario, as part of a random review, requests access to the notes taken on each class for the purposes of assuring quality of care, the instructor is obligated to allow them that access;¬†
  6. If the instructor becomes aware of abuse (including financial abuse) of someone in care at along-term care facility (e.g., nursing home) or retirement home, they are required to contact the Ministry of Health; or
  7. If law enforcement officers request access to the class notes because they believe the information will assist in finding a missing person, under the Missing Persons Act (2018), the instructor is obligated to allow them access.

In this course, students will be invited to share observations about their mindfulness practice through synchronous discussions in class. All students will also be required to share brief written reflections with the instructor and teaching assistant (TA) as part of the course assignments. Students should follow the instructor‚Äôs guidelines and prompts regarding reflection to maximize the chances that information shared is tied to the practice of mindfulness. Following these guidelines will also help to minimize the chances of students sharing overly personal, emotionally charged information that is less directly related to the mindfulness practice and that may have the potential to trigger distress in them and other students in the class. It is nevertheless possible that emotionally vulnerable material will arise and be shared. In all cases of sharing ‚Äì non-verbal, oral, and written ‚Äì the instructor and TA will keep what is shared confidential unless your explicit consent has been communicated or if what you shared requires the instructor to breach confidentiality as required by the College of Psychologists of Ontario, detailed above. It should be noted that although all students in the class will be encouraged to keep in-class discussions confidential, the instructor is not able to oversee what is done outside of class time and cannot enforce this broader confidentiality.¬†
