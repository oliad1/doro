Course Description

CS 251:

Overview of computer organization and performance. Basics of digital logic design. Combinational and sequential elements. Data representation and manipulation. Basics of processor design. Pipelining. Memory hierarchies. Multiprocessors.

View requirements for CS 251

Course Description:

This course enables students to develop an accurate mental model of the architecture and organization of physical computers and it introduces them to theoretical and practical concepts relevant to the structure and design of modern digital computers. The course also helps students understand and predict the behaviour and performance of computer programs executing on real machines.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Design simple combinational and sequential hardware at the logic gate level in order to implement simple algorithms and computations, such as addition, multiplication, and data-path control for processors.
Describe number systems used by computer hardware, including IEEE floating point and two's complement.
Explain the limits of the binary representations, including rounding error and overflow conditions.
Analyze how machine language is executed by hardware, and describe a simple pipelined processor architecture for executing a RISC machine language.
Explain basic cache and virtual-memory architectures, and how they can impact performance.

Tentative Course Schedule

Course Overview:

Introduction and ARM (~3 hours)
Overview of computer organization. Measuring performance.

Digital Logic Design (~8 hours)
Gates, truth tables, and logic equations. Combinational logic and basic components. PLAs and ROMs. Memory elements. Finite state machines.

Data Representation and Manipulation (~4 hours)
Signed and unsigned integer representations. Addition and subtraction. ALUs. Using finite state machines to control datapaths. Multiplication. IEEE floating-point representation.

Basic Processor Design (~9 hours)
Basic processor datapaths. Processor design using single-cycle control. Multi-cycle control. Microprogramming.

Pipelining (~6 hours)
Pipelined datapaths. Data hazards. Branch hazards.

Memory Hierarchies (~5 hours)
Caches: direct-mapped, fully-associative, set-associative. Virtual memory. Page tables and TLBs.

Multiprocessing (~1 hour)
Single-bus networks. Cache consistency. Multi-processor systems and core processors. Networks and clusters.

Student Assessment

Component Value
(see below)

The grading scheme will depend on how much and what parts of the course are offered online vs offered on campus. The basic weights used for various components (assuming on campus offerings) are:

  • AW=20 for Assignments (7 assignments, A1=2, A2=3, A3=3, A4=3, A5=3, A6=3, A7=3)
  • PW=5 for Participation Marks (half for clicker questions during lecture and half for tutorial problems during labs)
  • MW=25 for Midterm
  • FW=50 for Final Exam

Participation Marks are a combination of clicker questions asked during lectures and tutorial problems that are to be submitted after labs. Potentially up to 20% of your lowest clicker question marks and 20% of your lowest tutorial problems may be dropped (see the Switch to Online Guidelines below).

Note: Students may only enrol for answering clicker questions in their own registered section. Students registering their clicker for other sections will receive a grade of zero on clicker questions.

If the course has to switch online (due to public health guidelines), clicker question marks may be replaced with online quizzes.

If the Final Exam is offered online, its weight will change to

  • FW=40 for Online Final Exam

See the Exam Information page for information about the Midterm and Final Exam.

Students are required to obtain a passing grade for the weighted average of the Midterm and Final Exam (see formula below).

We will apply the following algorithm to determine your final course grade (assuming the entire course is held on-campus):

  • Participation = 1/2*(Best 80% of Tutorial Questions) + 1/2*(Best 80% of Clicker Questions)
  • Normal  = (AW*Assignments + PW*Participation + MW*Midterm + FW*Final) / (AW+PW+MW+FW)
  • Exam    = (MW*Midterm + FW*Final) / (MW+FW)
  • if ( Exam < 50% )
    • Course Grade = min (Normal, Exam)
  • else
    • Course Grade = Normal

Administrative Policy

Attendance:

You are highly encouraged to attend all lectures and keep detailed class notes.

Posting Course Information and Materials

The course notes are posted in the protected section of the course website. Students are required to delete all course files at the end of the term.

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. Sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.

In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

The Secretariat has posted Guidelines concerning Chegg, OneClass and other similar businesses.

Should the course be required to switch online (possibly due to public health guidelines), videos may be posted. They are considered part of the course materials. All requirements on intellectual property mentioned above apply to them as well.
Online lectures may be recorded. Students attending online lectures implicitly acknowledge and consent to being recording during such lectures.

Assignment submission

Assignments must be submitted online on edX and/or Crowdmark.

Assignment returns

Graded material will be returned to students via edX and/or Crowdmark.

Late Assignments

In general, assignments submitted late and without an approved extension by the course ISC will not be accepted and will be given a grade of zero.

If you have an approved extension for your assignment, submit your assignment before the extension deadline.

Missed Assignments

An assignment not handed in receives a mark of 0, unless there is a documented reason. If a student makes a short-term 48-hour declaration, they may contact the course ISC to obtain a 48-hour extension. If another documented reason is supplied, an extension may be provided or the weight of the missing assignment may be shifted to the (weighted) exam component of the grade at the discretion of the course ISC. Please check the accommodations page for further information. A copy of the documented reason must be given to and approved by the course ISC.

Since assignments are posted in advance with deadlines stated in advance, no adjustments will be made for assignments missed because of co-op interviews.

Please note that no more than two assignments may be excluded from the final grade, regardless of your circumstances. If your circumstances are such that that level of accommodation is insufficient, consult an academic advisor to discuss your options.

Missed Exams

A missed Exam will receive a mark of 0, unless there is a valid documented reason. If a documented reason is provided for missing an exam, its weight will be shifted to the other exams or the student may have to take an incomplete in the course (determined by the course staff). Please check the accommodations page for further information.

Incompletes

If a student is in good standing in the course and a documented reason is provided for missing the Final Exam, a grade of incomplete (INC) may be given (to be determined by the course staff at their discretion).

The course ISC must be contacted immediately to verify the student's standing in the course and verify that the reason for missing the Final Exam is sufficient and valid.

If an INC is granted, the student's grade will be calculated using the weightings of the course components from the term the student was registered, the student's term marks from the registered term, and the marks from the assessments that are written in future terms to replace any missed assessments. The Final Exam is usually written with the scheduled exam in the following term.

Questions or Concerns about the Way Your Work Has Been Marked

You have up to one week after the assessment has been returned to request a remark.

For assignments, contact the course ISA. If you are not satisfied with the response, then you can take it to the instructor.

Note that we may remark the entire assignment, quiz, or exam, so you could receive a grade lower than the one originally assigned.

Cheating

We will usually use the standard university penalty for cheating on an assignment/quiz, which is 0 on the assignment/quiz question and a deduction of 5% from the course grade. (The final penalty is usually determined by the Academic Integrity Officer of the Faculty of Mathematics.) In addition, a letter will be sent to the Associate Dean of Undergraduate Studies informing them of the matter. Note that penalties are significantly higher for exams and for repeat offenders.

Rules for Group Work

Discussion of assignments is allowed, but each student must hand in their own copy of the assignment and word-for-word copying is not allowed. You should not search for assignment solutions online, copy solutions from previous terms, photocopy another student's assignment, or copy another student's answers word-for-word.

You should not discuss quiz questions or test questions with other students in the course until after the day the quiz/test is due. Solutions for quizzes or tests that are too similar will be considered cases of excessive collaboration.

In-Class Discipline

Students are encouraged to attend all lectures, but are required not to be disruptive during lectures out of respect for their classmates and for the instructor. Disruptive behaviour includes talking with people next to them, texting on a phone, playing YouTube videos, Facebook and Twitter updates, etc.

Please ensure that all phones are silenced during lectures, to avoid distracting other members of the class. Also note that comments and feedback about the course are welcome, but outside of lecture time.

A student who is found to be disruptive during lectures will be given only one verbal warning for the term. At the instructor's discretion, if the same student continues to be disruptive, for each incident of disruption, they will be penalized 3% of their final mark for the course, and may be asked to leave the lecture during which the disruption incident occurred. They would also be liable for any missed milestones during the lecture.

Group Discussion (Piazza)

General questions on Piazza should be posted publicly, so all students can read the answers.

If you need to contact the course staff about a situation specific to you (e.g., an assignment grade), please consider contacting the instructor or ISA responsible for the assignment by email, or talking to them during their office hours.

Switch to Online Guidelines

It is possible that circumstances may disrupt on campus teaching during the term. The following policies will be followed in this course.

  • Students should attend the section they're enrolled in and only the section they're enrolled in
  • Shift to Online
    Weeks in which the course is offered online, readings from the course textbook and videos will be used in place of lectures during this time. Further, a weekly quiz will be given and used in place of clickers in the marking scheme. Both the videos and the weekly quiz will be in Learn.
  • Dropping lowest clickers marks, lowest quiz score
    If five or more quizzes are given, then your lowest quiz mark will be dropped before computing your quiz mark. If 15 or more clicker questions are asked, the lowest 20% of your clicker marks will be dropped before computing your clicker mark.
  • Online Exams
    If in-person exams are not possible, the midterm and/or final exam may be administered as a combination Learn/edX/Crowdmark exam.
  • Illness/Quarantine
    The intention of dropping the lowest 20% of clicker question marks is to account for students who cannot attend classes due to personal circumstances (eg. co-op interviews, illnesses including COVID, etc.). If a student is forced to miss class for an extended period of time (at least two weeks), you can contact the course ISC for an appropriate accommodation.
    A missed midterm will have its weight shifted in the final exam. A missed final exam will generally result in an incomplete in the course (subject to university policy that you must be in good standing in the course).
    If the entire course is moved online during all or part of a quarantine period, then no accomodations will be made during this time.
    Should you contract COVID, an accommodation may be provided to you. For more details, please review the Accommodations page.

University Policy
